How to Strip Paint from Woodwork: Methods, Costs and When to Call a Pro

Stripping paint from woodwork is a common task for homeowners refreshing their interiors or preparing surfaces for redecorating. Whether you're tackling skirting boards, door frames or window sills, understanding your options can save time, money and frustration. This guide covers the most effective methods, realistic costs, and when professional help makes sense.

Chemical Paint Strippers: The Most Popular UK Method

Chemical paint strippers remain the most widely used option for removing paint from woodwork in the UK. Solvent-based strippers work by breaking down the paint's molecular structure, allowing you to scrape it away cleanly. These products are effective on multiple layers of paint and work well on intricate mouldings where mechanical methods struggle.

Water-based or eco-friendly strippers have gained popularity in recent years, particularly for indoor work. They're less toxic than traditional solvents, produce fewer harmful fumes, and are easier to dispose of safely. However, they may work more slowly and sometimes require longer contact time on stubborn paint.

Costs for chemical strippers typically range from GBP10 to GBP30 per litre, with most small projects requiring one or two litres. Application is straightforward: brush the product onto the surface, wait 15 to 30 minutes, then scrape away the loosened paint. Always work in ventilated areas and follow manufacturer safety instructions carefully.

Mechanical Methods: Sanding and Wire Brushing

Mechanical paint removal using sanders, wire wheels or hand tools offers a chemical-free alternative that many homeowners prefer for smaller projects. Hand sanding with coarse-grit sandpaper (60-80 grit) is labour-intensive but gives excellent control, especially around delicate details. Electric orbital sanders speed up the process significantly on flat surfaces like skirting boards, though they're less suitable for detailed work.

Wire brush attachments on power drills work well for removing paint from textured or moulded surfaces. This method produces considerable dust, so always wear a dust mask and ensure proper ventilation. The main drawback is the physical effort required and the time investment for large areas—a single room of skirting boards could take 8 to 12 hours of work.

Expect to spend GBP20 to GBP80 on sanders or wire brushes if you don't already own them. This method works best for paint that's already flaking or cracked and is ideal if you have respiratory sensitivities to chemical products. However, it's less effective on paint that's well-adhered to the wood.

Thermal Methods and When They're Worth Considering

Hot air guns and infrared paint strippers soften paint through heat rather than chemicals or abrasion. These tools work by raising the paint's temperature until it bubbles and lifts from the surface, ready to be scraped away. They're particularly effective on old, thick paint layers and work well on mouldings where detail preservation matters.

However, thermal methods require skill to avoid scorching the wood underneath, and they're slower than chemical strippers for large areas. There's also a burn risk if you're not experienced with the equipment. They're best suited to detailed work like Victorian skirting boards or ornate door frames where precision is essential. You can hire a hot air gun for around GBP15 to GBP25 per day, making them economical for single-room projects.

One significant consideration: thermal methods can release lead dust if you're working on properties painted before the 1980s. In these cases, chemical strippers or professional removal services are safer choices. Always check your paint's age and composition before deciding on this approach.

Safety Considerations and Best Practices

Paint stripping involves genuine health and safety considerations that deserve serious attention. Chemical strippers can cause skin irritation, respiratory issues and headaches if ventilation is inadequate. Always wear nitrile gloves, eye protection and a properly fitted respirator mask, not just a dust mask. Work outdoors or with windows and doors open, and take regular breaks in fresh air.

Lead paint poses particular risks, especially for households with children or pregnant women. UK homes painted before 1987 may contain lead, which causes serious developmental and neurological damage. Never sand, heat or mechanically disturb lead paint—this creates highly toxic dust. If lead is present, professional encapsulation or safe removal is essential. Your local council environmental health team can advise on testing and safe removal.

After stripping, dispose of materials responsibly. Chemical stripper residues may be hazardous waste; contact your local tip for guidance. Dust and paint flakes should be sealed in bags rather than scattered. Wear gloves when handling stripped surfaces, as residual chemicals may remain on the wood. Once stripped and cleaned, the wood needs proper preparation with primer before repainting—rushing this stage leads to poor adhesion and early failure.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to strip paint from woodwork?

Chemical strippers typically require 15 to 30 minutes contact time per coat, plus 30 to 60 minutes scraping. A single room of skirting boards and frames takes 6 to 12 hours with chemicals, 12 to 20 hours with sanding, and 8 to 16 hours with thermal methods. Professional decorators usually complete similar work in 1 to 2 days. Time varies significantly based on paint condition, number of layers, and woodwork complexity.

Is it cheaper to do paint stripping myself or hire a professional?

DIY costs GBP50 to GBP150 in materials plus your labour time, making it economical for small projects. Professional removal costs GBP400 to GBP800 per room but saves 20+ hours of labour and delivers superior results. For detailed or lead-painted woodwork, professional service is safer and often better value. Get quotes from local professionals to compare against your DIY time investment.

Can I use the same paint stripper on all types of paint and wood?

Most general-purpose strippers work on common paints, but oil-based, acrylic and specialist finishes may require different products. Always check the stripper's suitability on the container. Some woods like pine are softer and require gentler handling than hardwoods. Test any product on a hidden area first, and follow the manufacturer's instructions for best results and safety.

What should I do if my woodwork has lead paint?

Never sand, heat or mechanically disturb lead paint as this creates toxic dust. Contact your local environmental health team for testing and advice. Professional encapsulation or safe removal by trained specialists is essential. This is a situation where professional help from experienced decorators is not optional—it's a health and safety requirement.
