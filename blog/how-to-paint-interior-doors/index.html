How to Paint Interior Doors: Tips for a Smooth, Long-Lasting Finish

Painting interior doors is one of the most rewarding DIY projects you can tackle, transforming your home with minimal cost and maximum impact. Whether you're refreshing tired woodwork or creating a bold statement in your Manchester or Lancashire home, proper technique makes all the difference between an amateur finish and a truly professional result. This guide walks you through every step to ensure your newly painted doors look stunning and last for years.

Why Proper Preparation Matters

The difference between a mediocre paint job and an exceptional one comes down to preparation. Most DIY painters rush this stage, but spending time here will save you frustration later and add years to your finish. Start by removing the door from its hinges if possible, laying it flat on sawhorses or a work surface. This makes painting easier and ensures an even coat without paint pooling in corners.

Next, fill any gaps, dents or imperfections with wood filler, then sand the entire door with 120-grit sandpaper to create a smooth surface that paint can grip properly. Pay special attention to any glossy varnish or existing paint, which must be dulled down to allow good adhesion. Wipe away all dust with a damp cloth and allow to dry completely. If your door has panels or moulding, take extra care in these areas as they collect dust and require thorough cleaning for paint to adhere evenly.

Choosing the Right Paint and Primer

For interior doors in UK homes, a quality satin or eggshell finish paint provides the best combination of durability and ease of cleaning. These finishes are tougher than flat paints and handle fingerprints and minor scuffs without showing every mark like high-gloss would. Avoid using standard emulsion paint designed for walls; it simply won't wear as well on surfaces that get regular handling and traffic.

Always use a dedicated primer for doors, particularly if you're making a dramatic colour change or covering dark stains. A primer seals the wood, prevents tannins from bleeding through, and gives your topcoat a superior base to adhere to. For a typical interior door, you'll need approximately 500ml of primer and 1-1.5 litres of paint, costing between GBP15 and GBP40 depending on brand quality. Budget for two coats of topcoat to achieve full coverage and colour depth that looks professional.

Application Technique for a Flawless Finish

Using the right tools dramatically improves results. Invest in a quality 2-inch angled brush for cutting in edges and panels, plus a foam roller for flat surfaces, which leaves fewer brush marks than traditional rollers. Apply primer first in thin, even coats, working with the wood grain where visible. Allow primer to dry fully (usually 2-4 hours depending on product) before sanding lightly with 220-grit paper to remove any grain raise.

For the topcoat, apply paint in the same direction as the wood grain, using smooth, overlapping strokes rather than dabbing or heavy-handed pressure. Work panel by panel on raised-panel doors, completing the outer frame last to blend edges together. A professional tip: remove the brush from paint and use long, smooth strokes rather than repeatedly dipping and stroking, which traps air bubbles. Between coats, maintain reasonable ventilation but avoid draughts that can cause uneven drying. Drying, Curing and Reinstallation

Allow paint to dry to touch within 2-4 hours, but don't reinstall the door for at least 24 hours, and ideally wait 48-72 hours for a full cure. Fresh paint is vulnerable to dust, fingerprints and damage during this critical period, so set up a clean drying space away from household traffic. Keep the room well-ventilated but away from direct sunlight, which can cause uneven drying and colour variation.

When reinstalling your door, carefully rehang it and wait another week before regular use if possible, or at minimum three days. Newly painted doors can stick slightly as paint cures fully, so don't be alarmed by minor resistance when closing them. For doors in high-traffic areas or homes with young children, consider waiting the full week before subjecting them to daily wear and tear. Once fully cured, your door will develop its maximum hardness and durability. Many professionals in Lancashire recommend applying a protective wax or polyurethane topcoat to high-use doors like those leading to kitchens or bathrooms, which adds a GBP50-80 investment but extends the finish's lifespan by years.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The most frequent error is painting over glossy surfaces without proper sanding and priming. No amount of beautiful topcoat will adhere properly to a shiny, unprepared base, resulting in peeling within months. Similarly, many people attempt to paint doors while still hung on hinges, which makes accessing all surfaces difficult and often leads to drips and uneven coverage, particularly on the bottom edge.

Another common pitfall is skipping primer or using cheap paint that requires three or more coats to achieve decent coverage. While premium paint costs more upfront, the superior opacity means two coats genuinely covers, versus cheap paint that needs three or four thin coats and still looks patchy. Temperature matters too: painting in cold, damp conditions slows drying and can compromise adhesion. Ideally, paint interior doors when temperatures are between 15-25 degrees Celsius and humidity is below 85 percent. Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to paint an interior door?

The actual application takes 1-2 hours per door, but total project time including preparation, drying between coats and curing is 3-5 days minimum. Proper drying time between primer and topcoat, and between coats of paint, cannot be rushed without compromising quality and longevity.

What's the best paint finish for interior doors in the UK?

Satin or eggshell finish paints are ideal for interior doors because they're durable, easy to clean and hide imperfections better than gloss while still being wipeable. Avoid flat emulsion paint as it doesn't withstand handling and dirt as well as satin finishes do.

Do I need to remove my door to paint it properly?

Removing the door from hinges is highly recommended because it allows you to lay it flat, ensures even paint distribution without drips, and gives you access to all surfaces including the bottom edge. If removal isn't practical, take extra care with drop cloths and paint more slowly.

How much does it cost to paint interior doors professionally?

Professional painting typically costs GBP150-300 per door depending on condition, colour change complexity and your location in Manchester or Lancashire. This includes preparation, primer, two topcoats and reinstallation, guaranteeing a finish that lasts much longer than DIY attempts.
