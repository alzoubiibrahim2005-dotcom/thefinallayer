How to Fill and Fix Cracked Walls Before Painting

Cracked walls are a common problem in UK homes, especially in older properties across Lancashire and Manchester. Before you paint over these imperfections, it's essential to repair them properly to ensure a professional finish and prevent the cracks from reappearing. This guide will walk you through the process of identifying, filling, and fixing wall cracks so your newly painted surfaces look flawless.

Understanding Different Types of Wall Cracks

Not all wall cracks are created equal, and understanding what you're dealing with is the first step to effective repair. Hairline cracks are the finest type, typically caused by normal settlement or minor temperature changes, and rarely indicate serious structural issues. These appear as thin lines running through plaster or paint and are very common in Manchester and Lancashire properties.

Moderate cracks are wider, usually between 3-10mm, and may suggest slightly more movement or settling in the property. Large structural cracks that are wider than 10mm or expanding rapidly could indicate more serious problems like subsidence or foundation issues, and you should consult a surveyor before attempting repairs. Additionally, cracks that keep reappearing after filling suggest underlying movement that needs addressing at the source rather than just covering up the symptom.

Preparing Your Wall for Crack Filling

Preparation is crucial for successful crack repairs and a smooth paint finish. Start by cleaning out the crack thoroughly using a small wire brush or an old screwdriver to remove loose plaster, dust, and debris. For wider cracks, use a vacuum cleaner to suck out particles, as any remaining dust will prevent the filler from adhering properly and may cause the repair to fail prematurely.

Once the crack is clean and dry, you may need to slightly enlarge it to create a better key for the filler material. Use a putty knife or filling tool to undercut the crack slightly so the filler material locks into place. Dampen the crack with water using a spray bottle just before filling, but don't leave it soaking wet. This helps the filler bind better with the surrounding plaster. For very deep or wide cracks, consider using a flexible backing rod first to fill most of the depth, leaving about 5mm for your finishing filler.

Selecting and Applying the Right Filler

The UK market offers several filling options, each suited to different crack sizes and situations. For hairline cracks and minor surface damage, lightweight spackling paste or vinyl filler works well and typically costs between GBP3-8 per container. For larger cracks up to 10mm, use a multi-purpose filler or interior wall filler, which usually costs GBP5-12 and provides better coverage and durability. Flexible or paintable caulk is ideal for cracks that might move slightly, particularly useful in older properties where settlement is ongoing.

Apply your chosen filler using a putty knife, pushing it firmly into the crack to eliminate air pockets. For hairline cracks, a single application is usually sufficient, but wider cracks may need two applications. Allow the first layer to dry completely (typically 2-4 hours depending on the product) before applying a second coat. Sand the dried filler smooth using fine-grit sandpaper, ideally 120-180 grit, working in circular motions until the repair is level with the surrounding wall. Wipe away dust with a damp cloth and allow to dry before priming or painting.

Priming and Painting Your Repaired Walls

Once your filler is completely dry and sanded smooth, you need to prime the area before painting to ensure colour consistency and professional durability. Filled areas often dry slightly differently from the surrounding wall, so primer helps create an even base for your topcoat. Use a good quality primer suitable for interior walls, applying it with a small brush to the repair area first, then feathering it outward to blend with the existing surface.

Allow primer to dry according to manufacturer instructions, typically 2-4 hours, then apply your chosen paint colour. You may need two coats of paint to achieve even coverage, especially if the colour is significantly different from the previous finish. Use quality paint with good coverage properties, which costs between GBP15-30 per litre for interior emulsion but provides better durability and appearance. Sand between coats using very fine 220-grit sandpaper for professional results, and always work in good lighting to spot any imperfections before the final coat dries.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for wall filler to dry?

Most interior wall fillers dry between 2-4 hours depending on the product, room temperature, and humidity. Thicker applications take longer, so check the manufacturer's instructions on your specific product. It's best to wait overnight before sanding if possible, as this ensures complete hardness and easier sanding.

Can I paint directly over unfilled cracks?

Painting over unfilled cracks is not recommended, as the crack will eventually show through the paint, particularly with quality paint that doesn't bridge gaps. Additionally, moisture can penetrate unfilled cracks and cause the paint to peel or bubble. Proper filling ensures a professional finish and prevents future problems.

What causes cracks in walls?

Common causes include normal settlement as buildings age, temperature and humidity fluctuations, foundation movement, poor plasterwork, or structural issues. In older Manchester and Lancashire properties, settlement cracks are particularly common. Persistent or widening cracks should be investigated by a surveyor to rule out structural problems.

Should I fill cracks before or after priming?

Always fill and repair cracks before priming. Prime and paint should be the final steps after all repairs are complete and dried. This ensures the filler integrates properly with the wall and that primer and paint have a sound base to adhere to.
