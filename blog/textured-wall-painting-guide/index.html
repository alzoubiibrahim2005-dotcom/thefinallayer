How to Paint Textured Walls: Complete Artex and Rough Surface Guide for UK Homes

Textured walls and Artex finishes are common in older UK properties, but painting them can be tricky if you don't know the right approach. Whether you're dealing with bumpy Artex ceilings, rough plaster, or other textured surfaces, this guide will walk you through everything you need to know to achieve a professional finish. We'll cover preparation, product selection, application techniques, and when it's worth calling in the experts.

Understanding Your Textured Surface: Artex, Stipple, and Rough Plaster

Before you pick up a brush, it's important to identify exactly what type of textured surface you're working with. Artex is a branded textured coating that was incredibly popular in UK homes from the 1970s through to the early 2000s, particularly on ceilings. Stipple finishes, also called splatter or popcorn finishes, create a bumpy, uneven surface that can trap dust and make cleaning difficult. Rough plaster is simply plaster that hasn't been smoothed down, creating a naturally textured appearance.

Each surface type requires slightly different preparation and painting techniques. Artex and stipple finishes are particularly problematic because their uneven surface means paint doesn't apply evenly, and they can be difficult to clean before decoration. Many homeowners in Manchester and Lancashire are choosing to remove these finishes entirely, though this is time-consuming and messy. If you want to paint over your textured surface rather than remove it, understanding its composition will help you select the right primer and paint.

Preparation: The Most Critical Step for Success

Proper preparation is absolutely essential when painting textured walls and Artex surfaces. Start by thoroughly vacuuming and dusting the surface using a soft brush attachment or a damp cloth. Textured surfaces trap far more dust and debris than smooth walls, and any particles left behind will show through your paint and create an uneven finish. Pay special attention to the peaks of the texture where dust accumulates most easily. Once clean, allow the surface to dry completely, especially if you've used water-based cleaning methods.

Next, inspect the surface for any damage, cracks, or loose sections. With textured finishes, damage can be harder to spot, so run your hand gently across the surface to feel for areas that need repair. Minor cracks can be filled with flexible filler suitable for textured surfaces, which you'll smooth gently to match the existing texture. For more extensive damage or loose Artex, you may need professional assessment. Once repairs are complete, apply a quality primer designed for textured surfaces. This is crucial because textured surfaces are more porous and will absorb more paint, so a good primer ensures even coverage and reduces the number of topcoats needed. Budget around GBP15-25 for primer and GBP40-80 for quality paint for an average bedroom ceiling.

Choosing the Right Paint and Primers for Textured Surfaces

Not all paint is suitable for textured walls and Artex. You'll want to use a specialist textured surface paint or a quality vinyl matt emulsion rather than standard gloss or eggshell paints, which can emphasise imperfections. Textured surface paint is thicker and more forgiving, helping to fill minor gaps and creating a more uniform appearance. Some homeowners prefer matte finishes for textured surfaces because they hide imperfections better than shinier finishes, which can highlight shadows in the texture.

Primer selection is equally important. Standard primer won't adhere well to Artex or rough plaster because these surfaces are dusty and non-porous in places. Use a specialist primer for textured or matte surfaces, or a bonding primer that's designed to adhere to difficult surfaces. Water-based primers are becoming more popular due to lower VOC levels and easier cleanup, though oil-based primers sometimes provide better adhesion on very dusty surfaces. Painting Technique: Application Tips for Even Coverage

Applying paint to textured surfaces requires patience and the right technique. Use a high-quality roller with a medium or thick pile (GBP8-15) rather than a brush, as rollers are much better at working paint into the peaks and valleys of the texture. Start from the top of your wall or ceiling and work downwards in sections. Apply paint using gentle pressure and overlapping strokes, allowing the roller to do the work rather than pressing hard. This prevents the paint from pooling in the texture or creating uneven patches.

Make sure you use long enough strokes to maintain a wet edge, which prevents lap marks from showing. Work in small sections of about 1-2 square metres at a time, maintaining a wet edge between sections. Most textured surfaces require two coats of paint for good coverage, though this depends on the porosity of your surface and the quality of your primer. Allow adequate drying time between coats, typically 4-6 hours for water-based paints, though always check your specific product instructions. Don't be tempted to apply thick coats thinking it'll speed up the process; thin, even coats always look better and last longer on textured surfaces.

When to Call a Professional Decorator

While painting textured surfaces is certainly a DIY job you can tackle, some situations benefit from professional help. If your Artex or textured surface is very uneven, damaged, or extensive, a professional painter has the experience and equipment to handle it properly. Large ceiling areas can be particularly challenging because of the physical demands and the need to maintain consistent quality across a large space. Additionally, if you're dealing with older Artex that may contain asbestos, professional removal rather than painting might be the safer option, particularly if you're in Manchester or Lancashire where many older properties contain these materials.

Professional painters also have access to specialist equipment like spray systems that can sometimes produce better results on highly textured surfaces than traditional rolling. The cost of professional decoration varies depending on the surface area and condition, but expect to pay around GBP400-800 for an average bedroom ceiling or GBP1000-2000 for multiple rooms. Frequently Asked Questions

Is it safe to paint over Artex that might contain asbestos?

Artex manufactured before 2000 may contain asbestos fibres. If you suspect asbestos, painting over it is actually safer than disturbing it, as this seals the surface and prevents fibre release. However, if you need to remove it, have it professionally assessed first. Professional removal is available in Manchester and Lancashire from licensed specialists.

How many coats of paint do textured walls typically need?

Most textured surfaces need two coats of quality paint after a proper primer coat. However, very porous surfaces or darker previous colours might need three coats for even coverage. Always allow full drying time between coats for best results.

Can I use a paint sprayer on textured walls?

Yes, paint sprayers can work well on textured surfaces and are often used by professionals, but they require careful technique to avoid runs and overspray. For DIY application, a quality roller usually gives more control and better results unless you have experience with spray equipment.

What's the best way to clean textured walls before painting?

Vacuum thoroughly with a soft brush attachment, then wipe with a barely damp cloth to remove remaining dust. Allow to dry completely before priming. Avoid harsh scrubbing which can damage the texture. For stubborn marks, use sugar soap diluted according to instructions, rinsing thoroughly afterwards.
